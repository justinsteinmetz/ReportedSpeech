What Is Reported Speech?

Sometimes we want to tell someone what another person said. We have two ways to do this:

Direct speech — quote the exact words, using quotation marks:
"I work in a bank," said Daniel.

Reported (indirect) speech — describe what was said, without quotes:
Daniel said that he worked in a bank.

Notice two things changed in reported speech:

  1. The quotation marks disappeared.
  2. The verb work shifted back a tense to worked. This is called backshift.

Reporting Verbs & How to Use Them

Instead of always using say, English has many reporting verbs that add meaning:

saytellask believereplyrespond adviseagreechallenge claimdecidedemand inviteofferpersuade promiserefuseremind

For statements → use the reporting verb + that
She said that she was tired.

For questions → use the reporting verb + if / whether
He asked if I was coming.

For commands / requests / advice → use to‑infinitive
She told me to open the window.
The doctor advised me to eat more fruit.

Special case — "suggest": it cannot use a to‑infinitive. Use suggest + …ing or suggest that…

Miss Bell suggested talking to our parents first.
Miss Bell suggested that we talk to our parents first.

Command verbs (tell, order, instruct, direct, warn) are always followed by a to‑infinitive.

Key Rules to Remember

1. Backshift tenses one step back when the reporting verb is in the past.

2. Use that for statements, if/whether for questions, and to-infinitive for commands.

3. Suggest is special — never use it with a to-infinitive. Use suggest + …ing or suggest that.

4. In indirect questions, use normal word order (S–V) and drop do/does/did.
